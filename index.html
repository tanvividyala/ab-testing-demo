Note: This simulator uses dummy data for demonstration purposes only. The click-through rates are simulated to illustrate how A/B testing works. Every run draws fresh random visitors, so your results (and your classmates') will differ from run to run. That variability is part of the lesson.

In 2009, Google famously tested 41 shades of blue and found that a slightly more purple shade generated an extra $200 million in annual revenue. The original test was actually a multivariate test across multiple elements, not a simple A/B test. This simulator is a simplified demonstration for educational purposes.

Why a two-tailed test? Our hypothesis says the variant will get more clicks, which sounds one-directional. But this simulator still uses a two-tailed test, and that is the standard choice in industry: before running the experiment you genuinely don't know which direction the effect will go, and a variant that performs worse is just as important to detect as one that performs better. One-tailed tests are only appropriate when a difference in the "wrong" direction would be treated exactly the same as no difference at all, which is rarely true in product decisions.

Statistical vs. practical significance. With enough traffic, even a microscopic difference will eventually become "statistically significant." Significance only tells you an effect is probably real, not that it is big enough to matter. In practice teams decide up front on a minimum effect worth acting on (say, a 0.5 percentage-point lift) and weigh it against the cost of the change. Look at the size of the lift and its confidence interval, not just the p-value.

Sample size and power. The 30-day duration and 200 visitors/day were not arbitrary: real experiments compute the required sample size before launching, based on the baseline rate and the smallest effect they care about. With ~3,000 visitors per arm and a 3.5% baseline CTR, this test has roughly 80% power to detect a lift of about 1.3 percentage points. Smaller true effects will usually produce an "inconclusive" result, not because they don't exist, but because the test is underpowered for them.
